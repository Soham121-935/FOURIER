Q1 — EFS of cos(t) Lobes

Find the Exponential Fourier Series for \(x(t)=\cos(t)\) repeating with period \(T=\pi\).

Given Information

\(x(t) = \cos(t)\) for \(-\pi/2 < t < \pi/2\), repeating as full-wave cosine lobes.

1 │ ╱╲ ╱╲ ╱╲ │ ╱ ╲ ╱ ╲ ╱ ╲ │ ╱ ╲ ╱ ╲ ╱ ╲ │ ╱ ╲ ╱ ╲ ╱ ╲ 0 ┼────┬────┴──┬────┴──┬────┴─────▶ t -3π/2 -π/2 π/2 3π/2 |◄─ T = π ─▶|
📊 Interactive Wave Reconstruction Observe how adding complex terms (N) reconstructs the full-wave cosine lobes
Key Setup
$$ T = \pi \qquad \omega_0 = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\;\text{rad/s} $$ $$ C_k = \frac{1}{T}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} x(t)\,e^{-jk\omega_0 t}\,dt = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2}\cos(t)\,e^{-j2kt}\,dt $$
1
Expand cos(t) via Euler's formula

Replace \(\cos(t)\) with its complex exponential form so we can combine exponents:

$$ \cos(t) = \frac{e^{jt} + e^{-jt}}{2} $$

Substituting into the \(C_k\) integral:

$$ C_k = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2}\left[e^{j(1-2k)t} + e^{-j(1+2k)t}\right]dt $$
2
Evaluate each integral

Both integrals have the same form \(\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} e^{j\alpha t}\,dt = \frac{2\sin(\alpha\pi/2)}{\alpha}\). So:

$$ I_1 = \frac{2\sin\!\big((1-2k)\tfrac{\pi}{2}\big)}{1-2k} \qquad I_2 = \frac{2\sin\!\big((1+2k)\tfrac{\pi}{2}\big)}{1+2k} $$
3
Simplify the sines

Since \(1 \pm 2k\) is always odd, \(\sin\!\big((1\pm 2k)\frac{\pi}{2}\big) = (-1)^k\). This is because the sine of an odd multiple of \(\pi/2\) alternates \(\pm 1\).

$$ C_k = \frac{1}{2\pi}\left[\frac{2(-1)^k}{1-2k} + \frac{2(-1)^k}{1+2k}\right] = \frac{(-1)^k}{\pi}\left[\frac{1}{1-2k}+\frac{1}{1+2k}\right] $$
4
Combine the fractions

Add the two fractions with a common denominator:

$$ \frac{1}{1-2k}+\frac{1}{1+2k} = \frac{(1+2k)+(1-2k)}{(1-2k)(1+2k)} = \frac{2}{1-4k^2} $$
✦ Final Answer
$$ C_k = \frac{2(-1)^k}{\pi(1-4k^2)} $$
$$ x(t) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{2(-1)^k}{\pi(1-4k^2)}\;e^{j2kt} $$

for \(k = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \pm3, \ldots\)

Writing Tips
  • Always state \(T\) and \(\omega_0\) before anything else
  • Expand \(\cos(t)\) with Euler — this is the key step examiners look for
  • Show \(\sin\!\big((1\pm2k)\frac\pi2\big)=(-1)^k\) explicitly
  • Verify: \(C_0 = 2/\pi\) is the DC (average) value — sanity check!

Q2/10 — Full-Wave Rectified Sine

Find the TFS of \(x(t)=A|\sin(\pi f t)|\), a full-wave rectified sine with \(T=\pi\).

Given Information

\(x(t) = A|\sin(t)|\), period \(T=\pi\), \(\omega_0 = 2\) rad/s.

A │╲ ╱╲ ╱╲ ╱╲ ╱╲ │ ╲╱ ╲╱ ╲╱ ╲╱ ╲╱ │ 0 ┼─┴───┴───┴───┴───┴──▶ t 0 π 2π 3π 4π |◄ T=π ▶|
📊 Interactive TFS Reconstruction See how the summation of cosine harmonics builds the full-wave rectified sine

⚡ Symmetry Shortcut

This signal is even: \(x(-t)=x(t)\).

  • All \(b_n = 0\) — no sine terms needed
  • We only compute \(a_0\) and \(a_n\) — half the work!
1
DC component \(a_0\)

The average value over one period:

$$ a_0 = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi}A\sin(t)\,dt = \frac{A}{\pi}\big[-\cos(t)\big]_0^{\pi} = \frac{A}{\pi}[1+1] = \frac{2A}{\pi} $$

This makes sense — the average of a rectified sine is positive and about \(0.637A\).

2
Apply product-to-sum identity

For \(a_n\), we need \(\sin(t)\cos(2nt)\). Use the identity:

$$ \sin A\cos B = \tfrac{1}{2}\big[\sin(A+B)+\sin(A-B)\big] $$
$$ a_n = \frac{2A}{\pi}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi}\big[\sin\!\big((1{+}2n)t\big)+\sin\!\big((1{-}2n)t\big)\big]\,dt $$
3
Evaluate & combine

Since \(1\pm 2n\) are odd integers, \(\cos\!\big((1\pm 2n)\pi\big)=-1\), so each integral gives \(\frac{2}{1\pm 2n}\). Combining:

$$ a_n = \frac{A}{\pi}\left[\frac{2}{1+2n}+\frac{2}{1-2n}\right] = \frac{2A}{\pi}\cdot\frac{2}{1-4n^2} = \frac{-4A}{\pi(4n^2-1)} $$
✦ Final Answer
$$ x(t) = \frac{2A}{\pi} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{-4A}{\pi(4n^2-1)}\cos(2nt) $$
$$ = \frac{2A}{\pi} - \frac{4A}{\pi}\left[\frac{\cos 2t}{3}+\frac{\cos 4t}{15}+\frac{\cos 6t}{35}+\cdots\right] $$

Key Insight

The rectified sine has period \(T=\pi\) (half the original sine period), so the fundamental frequency doubles to \(\omega_0=2\). The harmonics are at \(2\omega, 4\omega, 6\omega,\ldots\) — all even multiples of the original sine frequency.

Q3 — Periodic Square Wave

\(x(t)=+1\) for \(0\le t<2\), \(x(t)=-1\) for \(2\le t<4\). Period \(T=4\).

Given Information

Period \(T=4\), fundamental frequency \(\omega_0 = \pi/2\) rad/s.

+1 │ ████████ ████████ │ │ │ │ │ │ −1 │ ████████ ████████ ┼────┬────┬────┬────┬────┬────▶ t 0 2 4 6 8
📊 Harmonic Synthesis & Gibbs Phenomenon Observe how adding individual sine harmonics constructs a square wave

⚡ Symmetry Shortcut

This signal is odd: \(x(-t)=-x(t)\).

  • \(a_0 = 0\) — no DC component (equal positive and negative areas)
  • \(a_n = 0\) — no cosine terms at all
  • Only \(b_n\) needs computing — that's just one integral!
1
Set up \(b_n\) with split limits

The signal changes at \(t=2\), so split the integral:

$$ b_n = \frac{2}{4}\left[\int_0^2 (+1)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi t}{2}\right)dt + \int_2^4 (-1)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi t}{2}\right)dt\right] $$
2
Evaluate each integral
$$ I_1 = \int_0^2 \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi t}{2}\right)dt = \frac{2}{n\pi}\big[1-(-1)^n\big] $$ $$ I_2 = \int_2^4 \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi t}{2}\right)dt = \frac{2}{n\pi}\big[(-1)^n - 1\big] = -I_1 $$

Notice \(I_2 = -I_1\). This makes sense because the second half-period is the negative mirror.

3
Combine: \(b_n = \frac12[I_1 - I_2] = \frac12[2I_1]\)
$$ b_n = \frac{2}{n\pi}\big[1-(-1)^n\big] $$

For odd \(n\): \((-1)^n = -1\), so \(b_n = \frac{4}{n\pi}\).

For even \(n\): \((-1)^n = +1\), so \(b_n = 0\).

Only odd harmonics survive — this is a hallmark of square waves.

✦ Final Answer
$$ x(t) = \frac{4}{\pi}\sum_{\substack{n=1,3,5,\ldots}}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi t}{2}\right) $$
$$ = \frac{4}{\pi}\left[\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi t}{2}\right)+\frac{1}{3}\sin\!\left(\frac{3\pi t}{2}\right)+\frac{1}{5}\sin\!\left(\frac{5\pi t}{2}\right)+\cdots\right] $$

Gibbs Phenomenon

At the jump discontinuities (\(t=0,2,4,\ldots\)), the Fourier series always overshoots by ≈9% of the jump height, no matter how many terms you include. This is an inherent limitation, not a computational error.

Q4/13 — One-Sided Exponential

Find the Fourier Transform, magnitude, and phase of \(x(t)=e^{-at}u(t)\), where \(a>0\).

Given Information

\(x(t) = e^{-at}\,u(t)\) — an exponential that starts at \(t=0\) and decays with time constant \(1/a\).

  • \(u(t)\) is the unit step function: equals 1 for \(t\ge 0\), equals 0 for \(t<0\)
  • \(a > 0\) ensures the signal dies out, which means the FT integral converges
1 │●╲ │ ╲╲ │ ╲╲___ │ ╲╲____ 0 ┼─────────╲___________▶ t 0 time constant = 1/a
📊 Time-Frequency Uncertainty Viewer Time Domain x(t) = e⁻ᵃᵗu(t) [left] vs Frequency Domain |X(ω)| = 1/√(a² + ω²) [right]
1
Apply the FT definition

Since \(u(t)=0\) for \(t<0\), the lower limit effectively becomes 0:

$$ X(\omega) = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-at}\cdot e^{-j\omega t}\,dt = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-(a+j\omega)t}\,dt $$

The two exponentials combine into one — this is why Euler's form is so powerful.

2
Evaluate the standard exponential integral
$$ \int_0^{\infty}e^{-\alpha t}\,dt = \frac{1}{\alpha} \quad\text{when Re}(\alpha)>0 $$

Here \(\alpha = a + j\omega\), and \(\text{Re}(\alpha) = a > 0\) ✓, so it converges.

$$ X(\omega) = \left[\frac{e^{-(a+j\omega)t}}{-(a+j\omega)}\right]_0^{\infty} = \frac{0 - 1}{-(a+j\omega)} = \frac{1}{a+j\omega} $$
3
Magnitude spectrum

For a complex number in the denominator, \(|1/(a{+}j\omega)| = 1/|a{+}j\omega|\):

$$ |X(\omega)| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{a^2+\omega^2}} $$

This is a Lorentzian-shaped curve, peaked at \(\omega=0\) with value \(1/a\), and the −3 dB bandwidth is at \(\omega = \pm a\).

4
Phase spectrum

The phase of \(1/(a{+}j\omega)\) is the negative of the phase of the denominator:

$$ \angle X(\omega) = -\tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{\omega}{a}\right) $$

Phase goes from \(+\pi/2\) at \(\omega\to -\infty\) through 0 at \(\omega=0\) to \(-\pi/2\) at \(\omega\to +\infty\).

✦ Final Answer
$$ X(\omega) = \frac{1}{a+j\omega} $$
$$ |X(\omega)| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{a^2+\omega^2}} \qquad \angle X(\omega) = -\tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{\omega}{a}\right) $$
Physical Intuition

Faster decay → wider spectrum. A large \(a\) means the signal dies quickly in time, so its energy is spread across a wider range of frequencies. A small \(a\) means a slowly decaying signal that concentrates energy near DC (\(\omega\approx 0\)).

This is the time-frequency uncertainty principle in action: you can't be narrow in both time and frequency.

Q5/14/17 — Double-Sided Exponential

Find the FT of \(x(t)=e^{-a|t|}\) (with \(a>0\), often \(a=1\)).

Given Information

\(x(t) = e^{-a|t|}\) — an exponential decaying in both directions from a peak at \(t=0\).

___ ╱ ╲ peak = 1 at t = 0 ╱ ╲ ____╱ ╲____ decays as e^(-a|t|) ─────┼─────────┼─────▶ t 0 symmetric about t = 0 (even function)
📊 Bilateral Decay Fourier Transform Time Domain x(t) = e⁻ᵃˡᵗˡ [left] vs Frequency Domain |X(ω)| = 2a/(a² + ω²) [right]

⚡ Symmetry Shortcut

This is an even function (\(e^{-a|t|} = e^{-a|-t|}\)), so its Fourier Transform is purely real — no imaginary part, and the phase is either 0 or \(\pi\).

1
Split the absolute value

The absolute value means different behavior for \(t<0\) and \(t>0\):

$$ X(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{0}e^{at}\cdot e^{-j\omega t}\,dt + \int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-at}\cdot e^{-j\omega t}\,dt $$
2
Evaluate each half
$$ \text{Left half: } \int_{-\infty}^{0}e^{(a-j\omega)t}\,dt = \frac{1}{a-j\omega} $$ $$ \text{Right half: } \int_0^{\infty}e^{-(a+j\omega)t}\,dt = \frac{1}{a+j\omega} $$

Each half is just the one-sided exponential FT from Q4, but mirrored!

3
Add the two halves
$$ X(\omega) = \frac{1}{a-j\omega}+\frac{1}{a+j\omega} = \frac{(a+j\omega)+(a-j\omega)}{(a-j\omega)(a+j\omega)} = \frac{2a}{a^2+\omega^2} $$

The \(j\omega\) terms cancel perfectly, leaving a purely real result. This confirms the even-symmetry prediction.

✦ Final Answer
$$ X(\omega) = \frac{2a}{a^2+\omega^2} $$
$$ \text{For }a=1: \quad X(\omega) = \frac{2}{1+\omega^2} $$

Magnitude = \(\frac{2a}{a^2+\omega^2}\), Phase = 0 for all \(\omega\) (purely real and positive).

Connection to Q4

Notice how \(e^{-a|t|}\) is just two copies of \(e^{-at}u(t)\) placed back-to-back. Its FT is literally the sum of the Q4 result and its time-reversed version. This is the linearity + time-reversal property at work.

Q6/16 — FT of cos(Ω₀t)

Find the Fourier Transform of a pure cosine \(x(t) = \cos(\Omega_0 t)\).

Given Information

\(x(t) = \cos(\Omega_0 t)\) — a pure sinusoid lasting from \(-\infty\) to \(+\infty\).

This signal has infinite energy (it never dies out), so the FT doesn't converge in the ordinary sense. We need impulse functions (Dirac deltas).

📊 Pure Sinusoid & Spectral Spikes Time Domain x(t) = cos(Ω₀t) [left] vs Frequency Domain X(ω) = π[δ(ω - Ω₀) + δ(ω + Ω₀)] [right]
1
Start from the known FT of a delta

We know the inverse FT pair:

$$ \mathcal{F}\{1\} = 2\pi\,\delta(\omega) \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \mathcal{F}^{-1}\{2\pi\,\delta(\omega-\Omega_0)\} = e^{j\Omega_0 t} $$

This tells us: \(\mathcal{F}\{e^{j\Omega_0 t}\} = 2\pi\,\delta(\omega - \Omega_0)\).

2
Express cosine via Euler's formula
$$ \cos(\Omega_0 t) = \frac{e^{j\Omega_0 t}+e^{-j\Omega_0 t}}{2} $$
3
Apply linearity of FT
$$ X(\omega) = \frac{1}{2}\cdot 2\pi\,\delta(\omega-\Omega_0) + \frac{1}{2}\cdot 2\pi\,\delta(\omega+\Omega_0) $$
✦ Final Answer
$$ X(\omega) = \pi\big[\delta(\omega-\Omega_0)+\delta(\omega+\Omega_0)\big] $$

Two impulses of strength \(\pi\), located at \(\omega = \pm\Omega_0\).

Spectrum Sketch
X(ω) │ π │ ↑ ↑ │ │ │ │ │ │ ────┼────┼────────────────────┼────▶ ω -Ω₀ 0 +Ω₀ Two delta spikes of height π at ±Ω₀ Phase = 0 (both are real and positive)
Why This Matters

A pure cosine contains energy at exactly one frequency (plus its negative mirror). The Dirac deltas are the mathematical way to say "all the energy is concentrated at a single point in the frequency axis." This is the foundation of spectral analysis — real signals are combinations of these pure tones.

Q7 — TFS vs EFS Methods

Compare the Trigonometric and Exponential Fourier Series representations.

The Two Representations
$$ \textbf{TFS: } x(t) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\big[a_n\cos(n\omega_0 t)+b_n\sin(n\omega_0 t)\big] $$ $$ \textbf{EFS: } x(t) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}C_k\,e^{jk\omega_0 t} $$

Both are mathematically equivalent — they're just different ways of writing the same decomposition.

Side-by-Side Comparison
FeatureTFS (Trigonometric)EFS (Exponential)
Basis functions\(\cos(n\omega_0 t),\;\sin(n\omega_0 t)\)\(e^{jk\omega_0 t}\)
Summation range\(n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\) (one-sided)\(k = -\infty \text{ to } +\infty\) (two-sided)
CoefficientsReal: \(a_0, a_n, b_n\) (three sets)Complex: \(C_k\) (one set)
Coefficient count3 integrals per harmonic1 integral per harmonic
Physical meaningDirectly shows amplitude of sine/cosine componentsShows magnitude & phase in compact form
Symmetry benefitsEven → \(b_n{=}0\); Odd → \(a_n{=}0\)Real signal → \(C_{-k}=C_k^*\)
Best forExam problems, physical intuitionAnalysis, compact notation, FT connection
Conversion Between Forms

You can always convert between TFS and EFS coefficients:

$$ C_0 = a_0 \qquad C_k = \frac{a_k - jb_k}{2}\;(k>0) \qquad C_{-k} = \frac{a_k + jb_k}{2} = C_k^* $$

And in reverse:

$$ a_0 = C_0 \qquad a_n = 2\,\text{Re}(C_n) \qquad b_n = -2\,\text{Im}(C_n) $$
When to Use Which
  • Use TFS when the problem asks for "Trigonometric FS" or when you want to quickly identify which harmonics (cosine vs sine) are present.
  • Use EFS when the problem asks for "Exponential FS", when the algebra is simpler with complex exponentials, or when you need to connect to the Fourier Transform.
  • If no form is specified, EFS often involves less algebra since you solve one integral instead of two.
The Deep Connection

The EFS is the bridge to the Fourier Transform. As the period \(T\to\infty\), the discrete spectrum \(C_k\) at harmonics \(k\omega_0\) transitions into the continuous spectrum \(X(\omega)\). The spacing between harmonics, \(\omega_0 = 2\pi/T\), shrinks to zero, and the sum becomes an integral. This is how the FT naturally extends the FS to non-periodic signals.

Q8/15 — Gate (Rectangular Pulse)

Find the FT of the gate function \(x(t) = \text{rect}(t/\tau)\) — a pulse of width \(\tau\) centered at \(t=0\).

Given Information

\(x(t) = 1\) for \(|t| \le \tau/2\), \(x(t) = 0\) otherwise. Total width = \(\tau\).

1 │ ┌──────────┐ │ │ │ │ │ τ │ 0 ┼────┘──────────└──────▶ t −τ/2 0 τ/2 A rectangular pulse of height 1, width τ
📊 Gate / Rect Pulse ↔ Sinc Spectral Duality Time Domain x(t) = rect(t/τ) [left] vs Frequency Domain |X(ω)| = τ|sinc(ωτ/2)| [right]

⚡ Symmetry Shortcut

The gate function is even and real, so \(X(\omega)\) is also purely real and even.

1
Apply the FT definition

Since \(x(t)=1\) only on \([-\tau/2, \tau/2]\):

$$ X(\omega) = \int_{-\tau/2}^{\tau/2} 1\cdot e^{-j\omega t}\,dt = \left[\frac{e^{-j\omega t}}{-j\omega}\right]_{-\tau/2}^{\tau/2} $$
2
Evaluate the limits
$$ X(\omega) = \frac{e^{-j\omega\tau/2}-e^{j\omega\tau/2}}{-j\omega} = \frac{2\sin(\omega\tau/2)}{\omega} $$

We used \(e^{j\theta}-e^{-j\theta}=2j\sin\theta\) to simplify.

3
Write in sinc form

Multiply and divide by \(\tau/2\):

$$ X(\omega) = \tau\cdot\frac{\sin(\omega\tau/2)}{\omega\tau/2} = \tau\,\text{sinc}\!\left(\frac{\omega\tau}{2}\right) $$
✦ Final Answer
$$ X(\omega) = \tau\,\text{sinc}\!\left(\frac{\omega\tau}{2}\right) = \frac{2\sin(\omega\tau/2)}{\omega} $$

Peak value at \(\omega=0\) is \(X(0)=\tau\). First zero at \(\omega = \pm 2\pi/\tau\).

Spectrum Sketch
X(ω) τ ─│─────╱╲ │ ╱ ╲ │ ╱ ╲ │ ╱ main ╲ sidelobes │ ╱ lobe ╲ ╱╲ ╱╲ 0 ┼╱──────────────╲╱──╲─────╱──╲──▶ ω │ 2π/τ 4π/τ │ The sinc function: main lobe + decaying sidelobes
The Gate ↔ Sinc Duality

This is one of the most important FT pairs in all of signal processing:

  • Narrow pulse (small \(\tau\)) → wide spectrum (main lobe stretches out)
  • Wide pulse (large \(\tau\)) → narrow spectrum (energy concentrates near DC)

In the limit: an infinitely narrow pulse (Dirac delta) has a flat, infinite spectrum — it contains all frequencies equally.

Q9/11 — Triangular Wave

Find the EFS of a triangular wave with period \(T=2\).

Given Information

\(x(t) = t\) for \(-1 \le t < 1\), repeating with \(T=2\), \(\omega_0 = \pi\) rad/s.

⚡ Symmetry Shortcut

This is an odd function (\(x(-t)=-x(t)\)), so:

  • \(C_0 = 0\) (no DC component)
  • \(C_k\) is purely imaginary
1
Write the EFS coefficient formula
$$ C_k = \frac{1}{2}\int_{-1}^{1} t\,e^{-jk\pi t}\,dt $$
2
Integration by parts

Let \(u=t\), \(dv=e^{-jk\pi t}dt\). Then \(du=dt\), \(v=\frac{e^{-jk\pi t}}{-jk\pi}\):

$$ C_k = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{t\,e^{-jk\pi t}}{-jk\pi}\bigg|_{-1}^{1} - \int_{-1}^{1}\frac{e^{-jk\pi t}}{-jk\pi}\,dt\right] $$
3
Evaluate boundary terms and remaining integral

Boundary term:

$$ \frac{1}{-jk\pi}\big[e^{-jk\pi}-(-1)e^{jk\pi}\big] = \frac{1}{-jk\pi}\big[(-1)^k+(-1)^k\big] = \frac{2(-1)^k}{-jk\pi} $$

Remaining integral:

$$ \frac{1}{jk\pi}\int_{-1}^{1}e^{-jk\pi t}\,dt = \frac{1}{jk\pi}\cdot\frac{2\sin(k\pi)}{k\pi} = 0 $$

Since \(\sin(k\pi)=0\) for all integers \(k\), the remaining integral vanishes!

4
Simplify
$$ C_k = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{2(-1)^k}{-jk\pi} = \frac{(-1)^k}{-jk\pi} = \frac{j(-1)^k}{k\pi} $$

We used the identity \(\frac{1}{-j}=j\) to clean up the expression.

✦ Final Answer
$$ C_k = \frac{j(-1)^k}{k\pi} \quad (k\ne 0), \qquad C_0 = 0 $$
$$ x(t) = \sum_{\substack{k=-\infty\\k\ne 0}}^{\infty}\frac{j(-1)^k}{k\pi}\,e^{jk\pi t} $$

Key Observation

The magnitude \(|C_k| = 1/(|k|\pi)\) decays as \(1/k\), which is the same rate as the square wave. Both have jump discontinuities. In general, smoother signals have spectra that decay faster — a triangular wave with no jumps would decay as \(1/k^2\).

Q12 — Fourier Transform Properties

The 12 essential properties that make the FT so powerful.

Setup

Assume \(x(t) \xleftrightarrow{\mathcal{F}} X(\omega)\) and \(y(t) \xleftrightarrow{\mathcal{F}} Y(\omega)\). These properties let you find transforms of modified signals without re-doing the integral.

1 Linearity

Scaling and addition in time → same in frequency.

$$ ax(t)+by(t) \;\leftrightarrow\; aX(\omega)+bY(\omega) $$

2 Time Shifting

A delay in time → phase rotation in frequency (magnitude unchanged).

$$ x(t-t_0) \;\leftrightarrow\; e^{-j\omega t_0}X(\omega) $$

3 Frequency Shifting

Multiplying by a complex exponential → shift in frequency.

$$ e^{j\omega_0 t}x(t) \;\leftrightarrow\; X(\omega-\omega_0) $$

4 Time Scaling

Compressing in time → expanding in frequency (and vice versa).

$$ x(at) \;\leftrightarrow\; \frac{1}{|a|}X\!\left(\frac{\omega}{a}\right) $$

5 Time Reversal

Flipping the signal in time → flipping the spectrum.

$$ x(-t) \;\leftrightarrow\; X(-\omega) $$

6 Duality

Swapping time and frequency roles.

$$ X(t) \;\leftrightarrow\; 2\pi\,x(-\omega) $$

7 Differentiation in Time

Each derivative brings out a factor of \(j\omega\).

$$ \frac{d^n x(t)}{dt^n} \;\leftrightarrow\; (j\omega)^n X(\omega) $$

8 Integration

Integration in time → division by \(j\omega\) (plus a DC term).

$$ \int_{-\infty}^{t}x(\tau)\,d\tau \;\leftrightarrow\; \frac{X(\omega)}{j\omega}+\pi X(0)\delta(\omega) $$

9 Convolution in Time

Convolution in time = multiplication in frequency. This is the reason we use the FT.

$$ x(t)*y(t) \;\leftrightarrow\; X(\omega)\cdot Y(\omega) $$

10 Multiplication in Time

Multiplication in time = convolution in frequency (÷ by \(2\pi\)).

$$ x(t)\cdot y(t) \;\leftrightarrow\; \frac{1}{2\pi}X(\omega)*Y(\omega) $$

11 Parseval's Theorem

Total energy is the same whether measured in time or frequency.

$$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|x(t)|^2\,dt = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|X(\omega)|^2\,d\omega $$

12 Conjugate Symmetry

For real signals, the spectrum is conjugate-symmetric.

$$ x(t)\text{ real} \;\Rightarrow\; X(-\omega)=X^*(\omega) $$

Meaning: \(|X(-\omega)|=|X(\omega)|\), \(\angle X(-\omega)=-\angle X(\omega)\).

Most Tested Properties
  • Linearity + Time/Frequency Shift: Almost always used together in derivations
  • Time-domain convolution: The #1 reason Fourier analysis exists in engineering
  • Parseval's theorem: Used whenever energy calculations come up
  • Duality: Lets you get "free" FT pairs from known ones
